\section{Hypersonic Modeling\label{ch3:hypersonicModeling}}

In hypersonic flows, the fluid can no longer be assumed to be in chemical and
thermal equilibrium. Consequently, the \gls{NS} equations must be
modified to account for nonequilibrium effects. \Cref{eq:navierStokesNoneq}
presents the multicomponent \gls{NS} equations, which involve $4N+19$ unknowns
\cite{Daily2018}.
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:navierStokesNoneq}
    \begin{align}
        &\continuityEins \label{eq:noneqSpeciesContinuity}\\
        &\noneqSpeciesContinuityEins \label{eq:speciesContinuityEinsNoneq} \\
        &\noneqMomentumEins \label{eq:noneqMomentum} \\
        &\noneqEnergyEins \label{eq:noneqEnegy}
    \end{align}
\end{subequations}

The multicomponent \gls{NS} equations can be solved using:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single-temperature $(1T)$ models:} All energy modes
        (translational, rotational, vibrational and electronic) are in
    equilibrium and are described by a single temperature. Low fidelity, low
        computational cost.  
    \item \textbf{Two-temperature $(2T)$ models:} These models treat the
        vibrational energy separately, assuming rotational and translational
        energy equilibrates faster than vibrational energy. Medium fidelity,
        low computational cost.
    \item \textbf{Multi-temperature $(nT)$ models:} These models describe each
        energy level with a different temperature. High fidelity, medium computational cost.
    \item \textbf{State to state $(StS)$ models:} Solves the governing
        equations coupled to the master equation that accounts for the
        state-to-state transition \cite{Josyula2017}. Highest fidelity, highest
        computational cost.
\end{itemize}

The Park $2T$ model \cite{Park1989} is employed to investigate \gls{AO} properties
in nonequilibrium flows. This model assumes that the flow can be characterized by
two distinct temperatures: the translational-rotational temperature $(T_{tr})$,
which represents the energy associated with the translational and rotational
modes; and the vibrational-electronic temperature $(T_{vib})$, which accounts
for the vibrational, and electronic energy modes. This two-temperature
formulation enables a more accurate description of thermochemical
nonequilibrium, which becomes significant at the high enthalpies characteristic
of hypersonic regimes. \\

To numerically solve the nonequilibrium governing equations incorporating these
thermochemical effects, the open-source \gls{CFD} solver \Gls{SU2}--\Gls{NEMO}
\cite{Maier2023a} is used. \Gls{SU2}--\Gls{NEMO} extends the \Gls{SU2}
framework to model continuum flows in thermochemical nonequilibrium by solving
the multicomponent Navier–Stokes equations with appropriate source terms for
chemical reactions and internal energy relaxation. These equations are
expressed compactly in conservation form in
\cref{eq:nonequilibriumGoverningEqConservativeA} and expanded component-wise in
\cref{eq:nonequilibriumGoverningEqConservativeB}. The solver incorporates
detailed thermodynamic models, finite-rate chemical kinetics, and energy
exchange mechanisms, making it suitable for predicting both fluid dynamic and
