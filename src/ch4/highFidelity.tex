\section{High Fidelity Optics \label{sec:highFidelity}}
\subsection{\acrfull{StS}\label{sec:sts}}
\gls{StS} simulations were run for a range of conditions relevant to hypersonic
flight conditions for pure oxygen. This included a freestream typical of
$60\Units{km}$ altitude at different Mach numbers $(5 - 8)$. The vibrational
population distribution evolution post-normal shock waves at $60\Units{km}$ is
shown in \Cref{fig:stsDistribution} for Mach $5,6$ and $8$ as a function of
time behind a normal shock using \gls{QCT} based \gls{StS} simulations.
Significant differences can be seen at higher vibrational states but these have
a significantly lower population concentration compared to the lowest energy
states.

Using the results from the \gls{StS} simuations and the \textit{Buldakov
approach} \cite{Buldakov2000}, the vibrational-state specific polarizability
can be calculated, these results are shown in \Cref{fig:stsTemperatures}. Three
important trends can be obseverd. First, the polarizabilty is strongly
correlated with vibrational temperature and increases with time behid the
normal shock. This is important as sensors are often located dowstream of the
stagnation point. Second, as time increases, the difference between two
distributions increases for each Mach number before reacing a constant
difference. Third, the Mach $8$ case shows the larges difference between the
two types of distributions. While there is a strong dependence on the
temperature for polarizabilty, there is a weaker dependence on the
distribution (\textit{Boltzmann} vs. \textit{non-Boltzmann}). This is because
the population for each distribution is still dominant at the lower vibrational
energy leveles. While there is a larger difference seen in the higher
vibrational energy level, the population on these states are oder of magnitudes
smaller, so they have a small contribution to the overall polarizabiltiy.
