\subsection{Supersonic, High Enthalpy\label{sec:supersonicLES}}
The results presented below were obtained using the turbulent solver in
\gls{SU2}, as implemented and validated by Molina et al. \cite{Molina2017}.
In this work, Molina detailed the \gls{LES} formulation within \gls{SU2} and
performed validation studies against the canonical 2D backward-facing step
case from the \gls{NASA} Turbulence Modeling Resource database
\href{https://turbmodels.larc.nasa.gov/backstep_val.html}{https://turbmodels.larc.nasa.gov/backstep\_val.html}
at subsonic flow conditions. This benchmark case is widely used in the
turbulence modeling community because it provides experimental and numerical
data for separated flow, reattachment, and recirculation regions, which are
challenging to capture accurately with turbulence models. Additional
comparisons of \gls{SU2}'s \gls{LES} capabilities at subsonic speeds can be
found in Molina's subsequent presentation \cite{Molina2020}. \\

The conditions for this case are given in \cref{tab:lesStudy}. Due to the low
pressure, these conditions correspond to a high altitude of approximately
$26\Units{km}$. The flow is classified as high-enthalpy because of the high
velocity and temperatures within the flow; however, a real-gas model is not
used. Instead, a standard ideal-gas model is employed. \\

The dense-medium formulation of the index of refraction is shown in
\cref{fig:supersonicDenseIndexContourLES}. It was calculated using
\cref{eq:denseRefractiveIndex}, which computes the index of refraction as a
function of density and temperature. There does not appear to be a noticeable
difference between the dilute-medium and dense-medium formulations. The index of
refraction closely follows the density field; although the polarizability
follows the temperature field, its magnitude is $\mathcal{O}(10^{-28})$ smaller
than the density. Consequently, the index of refraction is more strongly
influenced by the density profile than by the temperature profile.

The \gls{OPL} (computed using the dilute-medium formulation) for a given time step is shown in \Cref{fig:supersonicOPL633}, and it was obtained from \cref{eq:OPL}, as implemented in the \gls{HAOT} package. A sharp increase in the \gls{OPL} occurs at approximately $-0.4\Units{m}$, which corresponds to the region just outside the shock. In this region, the density is significantly higher. Because the index of refraction is related to density through the \gls{GD} relation, an increase in density directly increases the local index of refraction. This, in turn, results in a larger optical path length and consequently produces a stronger contribution to the \gls{AO}.
