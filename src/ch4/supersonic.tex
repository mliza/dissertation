\subsection{Supersonic, High Enthalpy\label{sec:supersonicLES}}
The results presented below were obtained using the turbulent solver in
\gls{SU2}, as implemented and validated by Molina et al. \cite{Molina2017}.
In this work, Molina detailed the \gls{LES} formulation within \gls{SU2} and
performed validation studies against the canonical 2D backward-facing step
case from the \gls{NASA} Turbulence Modeling Resource database
\href{https://turbmodels.larc.nasa.gov/backstep_val.html}{https://turbmodels.larc.nasa.gov/backstep\_val.html}
at subsonic flow conditions. This benchmark case is widely used in the
turbulence modeling community because it provides experimental and numerical
data for separated flow, reattachment, and recirculation regions, which are
challenging to capture accurately with turbulence models. Additional
comparisons of \gls{SU2}'s \gls{LES} capabilities at subsonic speeds can be
found in Molina's subsequent presentation \cite{Molina2020}. The time step for this case is set to $\Delta t = 1\mathrm{E}{-5}$, the Vreman
\acrfull{SGS} model \cite{Vreman2004} is employed for \gls{SGS} turbulence
modeling, and a logarithmic wall model is used to account for near-wall
effects.\\

It must be emphasized, however, that the present case should be regarded
strictly as an experimental demonstration. The author does not recommend the
use of \gls{LES} within the \gls{SU2} framework for research or engineering
applications. At the time of this work, the \gls{LES} capability in \gls{SU2}
had not undergone sufficient verification or validation to ensure its accuracy
or reliability. Specifically, there has been limited systematic testing to
confirm the correctness of the numerical implementation, and unit testing for
the \gls{LES} functionality is either poorly documented or altogether absent.
Consequently, there is no basis to claim that the \gls{LES} methodology in
\gls{SU2} has been rigorously benchmarked against canonical turbulence cases,
nor that it can reproduce results consistent with established \gls{LES}
solvers. For these reasons, the use of \gls{LES} in \gls{SU2} at the time of
writing should be considered experimental only, and it is not recommended for
use in predictive simulations or for drawing quantitative conclusions. \\

The conditions for this case are given in \Cref{tab:lesStudy}. Due to the low
pressure, these conditions correspond to a high altitude of approximately
$26\Units{km}$. The flow is classified as high-enthalpy because of the high
velocity and temperatures within the flow; however, a real-gas model (e.g, one
that calculates chemical and thermal nonequilibrium as described in
\Cref{ch3:hypersonicModeling} and performed in \Cref{ch4:frozenVsNonequilibrium}) is not
used. Instead, a standard ideal-gas model is employed. \\

The mesh
and dimensions for case $L2$ are shown in \Cref{fig:supersonicMeshLES}. The
\gls{AO} properties were collected along a beam propagating in the normal
direction ($\hat{y}$), spanning from $-0.85\Units{m}$ to $0.0\Units{m}$. The
beam had a width in the streamwise direction ($\hat{x}$) ranging from
$1\Units{m}$ to $4\Units{m}$. The mesh was provided by the \gls{NASA}
Turbulence Modeling Resource database, and the Reynolds number was kept
constant to ensure suitability for the present flow conditions. A supersonic outlet boundary condition was applied, wall functions were used at the walls, and the same inflow conditions were prescribed at both the inlet and the far field.

The \gls{OPL} (computed using the dilute-medium formulation) for a given time step is shown in \Cref{fig:supersonicOPL633}, and it was obtained from \Cref{eq:OPL}, as implemented in the \gls{HAOT} package. A sharp increase in the \gls{OPL} occurs at approximately $-0.4\Units{m}$, which corresponds to the region just outside the shock. In this region, the density is significantly higher. Because the index of refraction is related to density through the \gls{GD} relation, an increase in density directly increases the local index of refraction. This, in turn, results in a larger optical path length and consequently produces a stronger contribution to the \gls{AO}.

As expected, the \gls{AO} properties are more pronounced in the higher-enthalpy
case. This is because higher enthalpy flows exhibit larger temperature and
density variations, which directly influence the aero-optical behavior.
Specifically, temperature fluctuations have a stronger effect on the
polarizability, while density variations predominantly affect the index of
refraction. Consequently, regions with strong thermodynamic gradients
contribute more significantly to the overall \gls{AO} distortions.
