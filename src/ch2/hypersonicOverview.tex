\section{Hypersonic Overview\label{ch2:hypersonicOverview}}
    The first hypersonic flight occurred in $1949$ at the White Sands Proving
    Ground during the early stages of the Cold War. The vehicle used was Bumper
    $5$ (see \Cref{fig:bumper5}), a combination of a slender, needle-like rocket
    called the WAC Corporal, mounted atop a V-2 rocket \cite{Anderson2022}. The
    WAC was developed in the United States, while the V-2 had been developed in
    Nazi Germany during World War II and was later brought to the U.S.,
    possibly as part of Operation Paperclip. 
    \pic{0.45}{chapter2/bumper5}{Bumper $5$, launched February $24$, $1949$
    \cite{NASA1950}}{fig:bumper5}

    In $1961$, Soviet cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin (\Cref{fig:yuriGagarin}) became
    the first person to safely reach space and orbit the Earth. During his
    mission, he also became the first human in history to travel at hypersonic
    speeds \cite{Anderson2022} during atmospheric rentry.
    \pic{0.1}{chapter2/YuriGagarin}{Yuri Gagarin, first person to reach
    hypersonic speeds \cite{YuriImage}}{fig:yuriGagarin}

        \subsection{Compressible Effects}
        As the velocity of the flow increases, compressibility effects can
        not be ignored. The flow field is typically characterized in five
        distinct regimes: incompressible, subsonic, transonic, supersonic and
        hypersonic \cite{Wilcox2010}.
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Incompressible:} A flow characterized by a constant
                density in which the density doesn't change significantly even
                when subjected to pressure variations.
            \item \textbf{Subsonic:} A flow characterized by a Mach number less
                than $1$ at every point. In this regime, compressibility
                effects can generally be neglected. The flow patterns in a
                subsonic flow are typically smooth and continuous, with no
                shock wave formation. Due to the absence of shock waves, these
                flows are often considered isentropic.
            \item \textbf{Transonic:} A flow characterized by a Mach number
                ranging between $0.8$ to $1.2$. In this regime, regions of subsonic and
                supersonic coexist; creating complex flow patterns and leading
                to the formation of localized shock waves. Compressibility
                effects cannot be ignored, leading to variations in fluid
                density.
            \item \textbf{Supersonic:} A flow characterized by a Mach number
                greater than $1$. In this regime compressibility effects
                cannot be ignored, leading to substantial variations in fluid
                density. Shock waves are present in this regime which produces
                discontinuous changes in pressure, temperature and density. 
            \item \textbf{Hypersonic:} A flow generally characterized by a Mach number
                greater than $5$. In this regime strong shock waves are
                present, leading to abrupt and significant changes in pressure,
                temperature, and density. Due to the high velocities, the
                kinetic energy in the flow is converted to thermal energy
                resulting in high temperatures. These high temperatures lead to
                \textit{real gas} effects and a thinner boundary layer.
        \end{itemize}

        At higher velocities, particularly in the supersonic and hypersonic
        regimes, compressibility effects are crucial to understanding the flow
        dynamics, and the simple models used at lower speeds are no longer
        adequate. \Cref{fig:ch2ShockMachRegions} show these regions, as the
        Mach number increases, shock waves start to form and compressibility
        effects become more important.

        \subsection{Gas Effects}
        In a hypersonic flow, the gas effects are typically called \textit{real
        gas} effects, which can be misleading. Four categories of gases can be
        identified \cite{Fletcher2004, Anderson2006}.
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Calorically Perfect Gas:} A gas with constant
                specific heats (\Cref{eq:caloricallyPerfectHeatCapP,eq:caloricallyPerfectHeatCapV}) in which the enthalpy
                and internal energy (\Cref{eq:caloricallyPerfectGasEnthalpy,eq:caloricallyPerfectGasEnergy})
                are functions of temperature. 
            \begin{subequations}\label{eq:caloricallyPerfectGasAssumptions}
                \begin{align}
                    c_p &= \text{constant} \label{eq:caloricallyPerfectHeatCapP} \\
                    c_v &= \text{constant} \label{eq:caloricallyPerfectHeatCapV} \\
                    h & = c_p T \label{eq:caloricallyPerfectGasEnthalpy} \\
                    e & = c_v T \label{eq:caloricallyPerfectGasEnergy} 
                \end{align}
            \end{subequations}
            The \textit{perfect gas} equation of state (\Cref{eq:equationOfState}) holds.
            \begin{equation}\label{eq:equationOfState}
                pv = R T 
            \end{equation}
            \item \textbf{Thermally Perfect Gas:} A gas where specific heats (\Cref{eq:thermallyPerfectHeatCapP,eq:thermallyPerfectHeatCapV})
                are functions of temperature only, in which the enthalpy and
                internal energy (\Cref{eq:thermallyPerfectGasEnthalpy,eq:thermallyPerfectGasEnergy})
                are functions of temperature. The \textit{perfect gas} equation of state
                (\Cref{eq:equationOfState}) holds.
            \begin{subequations}\label{eq:caloricallyPerfectGasAssumptions}
                \begin{align}
                    c_p &= c_p(T) \label{eq:thermallyPerfectHeatCapP} \\
                    c_v &= c_v(T) \label{eq:thermallyPerfectHeatCapV} \\
                    h & = h(T) \label{eq:thermallyPerfectGasEnthalpy} \\
                    e & = e(T) \label{eq:thermallyPerfectGasEnergy} 
                \end{align}
            \end{subequations}
            \item \textbf{Chemically Reacting Mixture of Perfect Gases:} A
                multispecies, chemically reacting gas in which intermolecular
                forces are neglected and each individual species obeys the
                \textit{perfect gas} law \Cref{eq:equationOfState}. The heat
                capacities
                (\Cref{eq:chemicallyReactingHeatCapP,eq:chemicallyReactingHeatCapV})
                not only depend on temperature but also in the number of
                particles per chemical specie. The enthalpy and internal energy
                (\Cref{eq:chemicallyReactingGasEnthalpy,eq:chemicallyReactingGasEnergy}
                are functions of temperature and number of particles per
                chemical specie.
            \begin{subequations}\label{eq:chemicallyReactingGasAssumptions}
                \begin{align}
                    c_p &= c_p(T,N_1,N_2,...,N_n) \label{eq:chemicallyReactingHeatCapP} \\
                    c_v &= c_v(T,N_1,N_2,...,N_n) \label{eq:chemicallyReactingHeatCapV} \\
                    h & = h(T,N_1,N_2,...,N_n) \label{eq:chemicallyReactingGasEnthalpy} \\
                    e & = e(T,N_1,N_2,...,N_n) \label{eq:chemicallyReactingGasEnergy} 
                \end{align}
            \end{subequations}
            \item \textbf{Real Gas:} A gas in which the effect of
                intermolecular forces is considered. Specific heats
                (\Cref{eq:realHeatCapP,eq:realHeatCapV}) are functions of
                temperature and pressure or volume. Due to intermolecular
                forces, the enthalpy and internal energy
                (\Cref{eq:realGasEnthalpy,eq:realGasEnergy}) are functions of
                temperature and pressure or volume. 
            \begin{subequations}\label{eq:realGasAssumptions}
                \begin{align}
                    c_p &= c_p(T,p) \label{eq:realHeatCapP} \\
                    c_v &= c_v(T,v) \label{eq:realHeatCapV} \\
                    h & = h(T,p) \label{eq:realGasEnthalpy} \\
                    e & = e(T,v) \label{eq:realGasEnergy} 
                \end{align}
            \end{subequations}
            For this gas, the \textit{perfect gas} equation
            \Cref{eq:equationOfState} is no longer valid, and a real gas
            equation, such as the \textit{van der Waals} equation
            \Cref{eq:vanDerWallsEquation}, can be used instead. 
            \begin{equation}\label{eq:vanDerWallsEquation}
                \Parenthesis{p + \frac{a}{v^2}}\Parenthesis{v-b} = RT 
            \end{equation}
            The parameters
            $a$ and $b$ are constants that depend on the specific gas under
            consideration.\\

            The \textit{van der Waals} model, makes two modifications to the ideal gas equation
            \Cref{eq:equationOfState}, adding a term to the pressure and
            subtracting a term to the volume. The term modifying the pressure,
            accounts for the short-range attractive forces between molecules
            that are not touching (potential energy
            repulsion). The term modifying the volume
            ensures that the volume does not go down to infinity. The constant
            $b$ represents the minimum volume occupied by a molecule when it is
            "touching" all its neighbors \cite{Schroeder2021}.
        \end{itemize}

        \subsection{Additional Degrees of Freedom}
        In hypersonic flow, where speeds exceed Mach $5$, the kinetic energy of
        the gas molecules is largely converted into thermal energy, resulting
        in extremely high temperatures. At these velocities, the chemical
        composition of the gas plays a critical role in the behavior of the
        flow. The high temperatures can cause significant molecular
        dissociation (the breaking apart of molecules) and ionization (the
        formation of charged particles) of air molecules. For example, at
        temperatures above $2000\Units{K}$, molecular oxygen begins to
        dissociate, and it is fully dissociated at around $4000\Units{K}$.
        Similarly, molecular nitrogen starts to dissociate above
        $4000\Units{K}$, and complete dissociation occurs at temperatures
        exceeding $9000\Units{K}$ \cite{Anderson2006}.\\

        At these elevated temperatures, the internal energy modes of the
        molecules are significantly altered. The Hamiltonian of the system,
        which represents the total kinetic and potential energy, must be
        modified to account for additional \glspl{dof}. These additional degrees of
        freedom correspond to internal energy modes such as rotational,
        vibrational, and electronic energies, which become increasingly active
        as temperature rises. These modes contribute significantly to the total
        internal energy and cannot be neglected in high-temperature
        environments, such as those encountered in hypersonic flows.
        \Cref{eq:hamiltonianEnergyExpansion} illustrates how the Hamiltonian
        can be expanded to incorporate these additional energy contributions.
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:hamiltonianEnergyExpansion}
            \mathcal{H} = \mathcal{E}_{kinetic} + \mathcal{E}_{potential} =
            \underbrace{\mathcal{E}_{tr} + \mathcal{E}_{rot} +
            \mathcal{E}_{vib} + \mathcal{E}_{elect}}_{\text{diatomic}} =
            \underbrace{\mathcal{E}_{tr}
            +\mathcal{E}_{elect}}_{\text{monatomic}} 
        \end{equation}

        This modification to the Hamiltonian is critical because the
        traditional energy models, which assume only translational motion; are
        insufficient to accurately describe the thermodynamic behavior under extreme
        conditions. In contrast, for supersonic flows, where temperatures are
        generally lower, the translational energy model is often sufficient to
        characterize the thermodynamic state of the gas.\\

        \Cref{fig:ch2AdditionalDoFs} illustrates the additional degrees of
        freedom that emerge in a hypersonic flow. As molecules absorb
        increasing amounts of energy, rotational and vibrational modes are
        excited, followed by electronic excitations and, at sufficiently high
        temperatures, ionization. These phenomena significantly affect the
        thermodynamic properties of the gas, including heat capacity, thermal
        conductivity, and viscosity, all of which must be accurately modeled to
        predict the behavior of hypersonic flows. 
        \pic{1.0}{chapter2/aditionalDoFs}{Additional Degrees of
        Freedom}{fig:ch2AdditionalDoFs}

        In addition, each newly accessible energy mode operates on different
        time scales, which significantly influences the thermodynamic response
        of the gas in hypersonic flows. These time scales are associated with
        the energy required for the excitation of rotational, vibrational, and
        electronic modes, as well as ionization to reach a \textit{steady
        thermodynamic state}. For instance, rotational modes
        tend to equilibrate quickly, while vibrational and electronic
        excitations, along with dissociation and ionization, occur over longer
        time scales due to the higher energy thresholds involved. \\

        \Cref{eq:timeScales} shows the specific time scales associated with each
        energy mode. 
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:timeScales}
            \tau_{flow} \approx \tau_{tr} < \tau_{rot} <
            \tau_{vib} < \tau_{chem}
        \end{equation}
        Understanding these time scales is crucial for accurately
        modeling the non-equilibrium thermodynamic processes in hypersonic
        regimes.\\

        The different time scales is the reason why flows at hypersonic
        speeds are in nonequilibrium. After air molecules travel across a
        shock, it will take certain number of collisions to convert the
        energy into thermal energy. In the case of translational energy is
        almost instantaneous, in the case of rotational is approximately $10$
        collisions and in the case of vibrational is approximately $1000$
        collisions \cite{Vincenti1975}. The nonequilibrium
        characteristics of the flow can be characterize by comparing the flow
        characteristic time with the other time scales \cite{Neitzel2017}. \\

These additional energy modes increase the number of degrees of freedom
(\gls{dof}) of the system, allowing molecules to occupy a broader range of
energy levels—including rotational, vibrational, and electronic states. As a
result, the partition function, which quantifies how particles in an ensemble
are distributed among available energy states \cite{Laurendeau2005} must be
modified to account for these added \glspl{dof}, as shown in
\Cref{eq:partitionFunction}. 
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:partitionFunction} 
            Z = \sum_{i}g_ie^{-\beta E_i}
        \end{equation}
In statistical mechanics, the partition function
plays a central role in determining thermodynamic quantities, including heat
capacities. These heat capacities, in turn, are critical for computing
macroscopic properties such as internal energy, enthalpy, temperature, and heat
transfer. In the context of fluid dynamics, particularly for high-enthalpy
flows such as those encountered in hypersonic regimes, these thermodynamic
properties strongly influence shock structures, boundary layer behavior, and
overall flowfield characteristics. The thermodynamic beta is given by \Cref{eq:thermodynamicBeta}
        \begin{equation}\label{eq:thermodynamicBeta}
            \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}
        \end{equation}

        \Cref{fig:ch2HeatCapacity} shows the change in heat capacity when
        additional \glspl{dof} are accessible at thermal equilibrium.
        \pic{1.0}{chapter2/heatCapacity}{Heat Capacity}{fig:ch2HeatCapacity}

        Under these conditions, the calorically perfect gas approximation
        becomes unsuitable, as it neglects key thermochemical effects that
        arise at high velocities \Cref{fig:caloricallyPerfectGas} illustrates
        the pre-shock and post-shock temperatures across various altitudes. As
        expected, at high speeds, the calorically perfect gas model
        underestimates the temperature increase across the shock.
        \pic{0.7}{chapter2/temperatureRatio}{Temperature ratio across a shock
        wave \cite{Anderson2004}}{fig:caloricallyPerfectGas}

        At elevated velocities, particularly in the hypersonic regime, the
        assumptions of constant specific heats and the absence of chemical
        reactions no longer hold. The gas molecules experience significant
        excitation, dissociation, and ionization, which are critical
        thermochemical effects that must be accounted for. These processes lead
        to non-linear increases in temperature and energy exchange across the
        shock, rendering the calorically perfect gas model
        insufficient \cite{Anderson2004}.  \\

        The thermochemical effects encountered in hypersonic flows are directly
        influenced by the interplay between the gas’s internal energy modes and
        the time scales of the flow. Whether the gas behaves in an equilibrium
        or frozen state depends on the relative rates of these energy exchanges
        and the flow dynamics. When the energy exchange rates are faster than
        the flow time scales, equilibrium can be achieved, allowing for uniform
        distribution of energy across all modes. However, when the flow
        dynamics outpace these energy exchanges, the gas is unable to
        equilibrate, leading to frozen behavior. This dynamic directly links
        the state of the gas to the complex thermochemical changes observed in
        hypersonic conditions, such as those caused by excitation,
        dissociation, and ionization. \Cref{fig:equilibriumFrozen} shows the
        difference between an equilibrium and frozen flow after a nozzle for
        the temperature and mass fraction.
        \begin{figure}[H]
            \makebox[\textwidth][c]{\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{chapter2/equilibriumFrozen}}%
            \caption{Frozen and equilibrium plots across a nozzle}
            \label{fig:equilibriumFrozen}
        \end{figure}
        
        \begin{itemize}
                \item \textbf{Equilibrium Flow:} A gas is said to be in
                    equilibrium when it has sufficient time to allow all its
                    internal energy modes—translational, rotational,
                    vibrational, and possibly electronic—to exchange energy and
                    reach a uniform state of equilibrium. In this condition,
                    the distribution of energy within these modes aligns with
                    the thermodynamic properties predicted by statistical
                    mechanics, such as the Boltzmann distribution. Equilibrium
                    assumes that the rate of energy exchange between modes is
                    much faster than the flow dynamics, ensuring the gas
                    behaves in a thermodynamically balanced state.
                \item \textbf{Frozen Flow:} Frozen flow occurs when a gas moves
                    so rapidly that its internal energy modes (translational,
                    rotational, vibrational, or chemical) do not have
                    sufficient time to equilibrate during the flow. This
                    happens because the flow time scale is much shorter than
                    the time required for internal energy exchanges or chemical
                    reactions to occur. In this state, the internal energy
                    distribution remains effectively "frozen" in its initial
                    condition, as reaction or relaxation rates are negligible
                    compared to the flow rate. Frozen flow is particularly
                    relevant in high-speed or hypersonic flows, where
                    non-equilibrium effects become significant.
        \end{itemize}

        \Cref{fig:ch2flightSpeedAltitude} illustrates the flight paths of the
        Apollo Command Module and the Shuttle Orbiter during reentry. The
        reentry trajectory spans a broad range of velocities and altitudes,
        making accurate trajectory prediction extremely challenging. As
        expected, the atmospheric density changes dramatically with altitude,
        significantly influencing the aerodynamic forces acting on the vehicle.
        These forces, in turn, affect the vehicle's overall trajectory.\\

        Atmospheric density is inversely related to temperature, and
        temperature variations directly impact the chemical composition of the
        surrounding air. As the vehicle descends, encountering higher
        velocities, the steep density and temperature gradients across shock
        waves become more pronounced. The faster the reentry vehicle, the
        sharper these gradients, which leads to complex shock-layer phenomena,
        including chemical reactions and ionization. \\

        During reentry, the vehicle passes through various regions of the
        atmosphere where different chemical reactions occur, significantly
        altering the surrounding air's composition. For instance, at high
        velocities and temperatures, air molecules such as molecular oxygen and
        nitrogen dissociate, and ionization occurs, creating a plasma sheath
        around the vehicle. This plasma sheath influences the vehicle’s
        electromagnetic properties, such as radio signal attenuation
        (blackout), which is critical for communication during
        reentry \cite{Parent2021, Parent2021a}. \\

        These chemical effects must be considered carefully, as they impact not
        only the vehicle’s aerodynamics but also thermal protection systems and
        onboard systems reliant on communication and navigation. \\
