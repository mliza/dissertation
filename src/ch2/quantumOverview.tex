\section{Quantum Mechanics Overview \label{ch2:quantumMechanicsOverview}}
   Unlike Newtonian mechanics, Maxwell's electrodynamics, or Einstein's
   relativity, \gls{QM} was not developed by a single individual
   \cite{Griffiths2018} but by multiple physicists,
   including Planck, Bohr, Heisenberg, Schr\"{o}dinger, Dirac, and others.
   To better understand the connection between hypersonic flows and optics,
   an overview of \Gls{QM} is necessary. \\

   As previously explained in \Cref{ch2:hypersonicOverview}, in a hypersonic
   flow, the \textit{Hamiltonian} \Cref{eq:hamiltonianEnergyExpansion} of a
   system must be modified. In molecular spectroscopy, the \Gls{BO}
   approximation is used to represent the total energy of the
   system \cite{Gupta2016}. The \Gls{BO} approximation assumes that the wave
   function of an atomic nuclei and electrons in a molecule can be treated
   separately, base on the fact that the nuclei are much heavier than the
   electrons \cite{Lindon2016, McNab2017} and it is shown in
   \Cref{eq:bornOppWaveApprox}.
   \begin{subequations}\label{eq:bornOppWaveApprox}
       \begin{align}
           \psi_{total} &= \psi_{elec}\,\psi_{vib}\,\psi_{rot}  \\
            \mathcal{H} &= \varepsilon_{elec} + \varepsilon_{vib} + \varepsilon_{rot}
       \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    In order to calculate the wave function $\psi$, the Schr\"{o}dinger's
    equation \Cref{eq:schrodingerEq} needs to be solved.s
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:schrodingerEq}
        \hat{H}\ket{\psi} = i\hbar\PartialFrac{}{t}\ket{\psi} 
    \end{equation}

   The vibration-rotation energy for a diatomic molecules approximated up to
   the 2nd order is shown in \Cref{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation}, and the
   general form using using the \textit{Dunham} energy coefficients is shown in
   \Cref{eq:rotVibrationalDunhamEnergy} \cite{Sun2002}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation}
        \resizebox{.9\hsize}{!}{$E_{v,J} = \underbrace{\omega_e \Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}} + B_eJ\Parenthesis{J +
        1}}_{\text{Harmonic}}  - \overbrace{\omega_e x_e\Parenthesis{v +
        \frac{1}{2}}^2 - D_e J^2\Parenthesis{J + 1}^2 }^{\text{Anharmonic}} -
        \underbrace{\alpha_e\Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}} J\Parenthesis{ J +
        1}}_{\text{Vibration-rotation interaction}}$}
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rotVibrationalDunhamEnergy}
        E_{vJ} = \sum_{m,n} Y_{mn}\Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}}^m
        J^n\Parenthesis{J + 1}^n
    \end{equation}

    Some textbooks \cite{Harris2008} use only the
    \textit{Harmonic} terms from \Cref{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation} to
    define the \textit{Hamiltonian}, which
    is a good approximation near the ground state. The vibrational energy for
    the \textit{harmonic} energy equation is given in
    \Cref{eq:vibrationalEnergy} for the vibrational term and in
    \Cref{eq:rotationalEnergy} for the rotational term. The additional terms
    come from perturbation theory and are called the \textit{anharmonic}
    terms \cite{Griffiths2018}.
    \begin{subequations}\label{eq:vibrationalRotationalEnergies}
        \begin{align}
            %\varepsilon_{tr} &= \label{eq:tranlationalEnergy} \\
            \varepsilon_{vib} &=
            \omega_e\Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}} \UnitsFrac{1}{cm} \label{eq:vibrationalEnergy} \\
            \varepsilon_{rot} &= B_e J\Parenthesis{J+1} \UnitsFrac{1}{cm}\label{eq:rotationalEnergy}
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    The \gls{ZPE} was calculated using \Cref{eq:zpe} provided in
    \cite{Irikura2007}. The \gls{ZPE} is the lowest possible energy that a
    quantum mechanical system may have. It has to be noted that this is only
    possible to be obtained for diatomic molecules, because monatomic
    molecules only have translational energy modes \cite{Eisberg1985}. 
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:zpe}
        ZPE = \frac{\omega_e}{2} - \frac{\omega_ex_e}{2} + \frac{\omega_ey_e}{8} +
        \frac{B_e}{4} + \frac{\alpha_e\omega_e}{12B_e} +
        \frac{\alpha_e^2\omega_e^2}{144B_e^3}
    \end{equation}

    Some authors refer to the constants in \Cref{tab:spectroscopyConstant} 
    as the \textit{Dunham} coefficients \cite{Dunham1932} $\Parenthesis{Y_{ij}
    = Y_{vJ}}$ \cite{Chandra2005}. However, in the literature, these
    coefficients may also be referred to as the \textit{Dunham} potential
    coefficients, which are not the same. Ogilvie \cite{Ogilvie1976} called the
    $Y_{vJ}$ energy-coefficients and $a_m$ the \textit{Dunham}
    potential-coefficients. These conventions will be used for the remainder
    of this work.\Cref{eq:dunhamEnergyCoefficients} provides a relationship between the

            Y_{00} &= ZPE   &  Y_{02} &= D_e        & Y_{11} &= -\alpha_e   &

            Y_{01} &= B_e   &  Y_{10} &= \omega_e   & Y_{20} &= -\omega_ex_e &  \\

    \subsection{Statistical mechanics}
    Statistical mechanics is fundamentally important for understanding
    macroscopic (macro) properties of matter because it provides the link
    between microscopic behavior (individual atoms and molecules) and the
    observable bulk properties of materials. \\

    In the case of \gls{AO}, statistical mechanics offers the critical
    framework that connects the microscopic behavior described by \gls{QM} to the
    macroscopic \gls{EM} properties relevant to optical signal propagation. At
    the microscopic scale, individual atoms and molecules possess discrete
    energy levels - including electronic, vibrational and rotational states -
    whose populations are governed by \gls{QM}. However, in a macroscopic
    system, it is impractical to track each particle individually. Instead,
    statistical mechanics provides a probabilistic treatment of a large
    ensemble of particles, enabling the prediction of bulk properties from
    microscopic laws.\\ 

A system reaches thermodynamic equilibrium when its entropy is maximized.
Entropy, fundamentally, is a measure of the number of microscopic
configurations (microstates) that correspond to a given macroscopic state
\cite{Reichl2016}. At low vibrational and rotational quantum numbers, the
system's entropy is lower because fewer energy states are available for
population. In contrast, at higher quantum numbers, the number of accessible
"energy buckets" increases, allowing energy to be distributed among more
states, thereby increasing the entropy.\\

    The energy distribution of a system in thermal equilibrium can be
    mathematically described by the Boltzmann distribution. This distribution
    provides the probability that a system occupies a particular energy state,
    demonstrating that higher-energy states are less probable, but become
    increasingly populated as temperature rises \cite{Callen1991}.
    Consequently, the Boltzmann distribution is fundamental for explaining the
    relationship between temperature, entropy, and the statistical behavior of
    molecules across various energy levels. \\

Specifically for \gls{AO}, the Boltzmann distribution is applied to determine
how molecules populate different vibrational and rotational energy levels as a
function of temperature. The probability distribution used for this calculation
is presented in \Cref{eq:boltzmannDistribution}, forming a foundational step in
computing macroscopic \gls{EM} properties from microscopic energy state
populations.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:boltzmannDistribution}
        p_{v,J} = \frac{g_Je^{-\beta E_{v,J}}}{Z} = \frac{\Parenthesis{2J +
        1}e^{-\beta\Parenthesis{\varepsilon_i +
        \varepsilon_j}}}{\sum_{v,J}\Parenthesis{2J +
        1}e^{-\beta\Parenthesis{\varepsilon_v +\varepsilon_J}}}
    \end{equation}
 
    It should be noted that, in a system of harmonic oscillators, vibrational
    energy relaxes through a series of Boltzmann distributions. However, this
    behavior does not hold for anharmonic oscillators \cite{Anketell1974}. The
    assumption of harmonic behavior can be applied to the rigid rotor model
    \cite{Ott2000}. Therefore, for the purposes of this approach, it is assumed
    that the system consists solely of harmonic oscillators. As a result, the
    anharmonic terms in the vibrational-rotational energy described by
    \Cref{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation} will be neglected.
