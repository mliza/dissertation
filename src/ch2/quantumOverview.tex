\section{Quantum Mechanics Overview \label{ch2:quantumMechanicsOverview}}
   Unlike Newtonian mechanics, Maxwell's electrodynamics, or Einstein's
   relativity, \gls{QM} was not developed by a single individual
   \cite{Griffiths2018} but by multiple physicists,
   including Planck, Bohr, Heisenberg, Schr\"{o}dinger, Dirac, and others.
   To better understand the connection between hypersonic flows and optics,
   an overview of \Gls{QM} is necessary. \\

   As previously explained in \cref{ch2:hypersonicOverview}, in a hypersonic
   flow, the \textit{Hamiltonian} \cref{eq:hamiltonianEnergyExpansion} of a
   system must be modified. In molecular spectroscopy, the \Gls{BO}
   approximation is used to represent the total energy of the
   system \cite{Gupta2016}. The \Gls{BO} assumes that the wave function of an
   atomic nuclei and electrons in a molecule can be treated separately, base on
   the fact that the nuclei are much heavier than the electrons
   \cite{Lindon2016, McNab2017} and it is
   shown in \cref{eq:bornOppWaveApprox}.
   \begin{subequations}\label{eq:bornOppWaveApprox}
       \begin{align}
           \psi_{total} &= \psi_{elec}\,\psi_{vib}\,\psi_{rot}  \\
            \mathcal{H} &= \varepsilon_{elec} + \varepsilon_{vib} + \varepsilon_{rot}
       \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    Note $\psi$ is the wave function and in order to calculate it, the
    Schr\"{o}dinger's equation \cref{eq:schrodingerEq} needs to be solved.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:schrodingerEq}
        \hat{H}\ket{\psi} = i\hbar\PartialFrac{}{t}\ket{\psi} 
    \end{equation}

   The vibration-rotation energy for a diatomic molecules approximated up to
   the 2nd order is shown in \cref{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation}, and the
   general form using using the \textit{Dunham} energy coefficients is shown in
   \cref{eq:rotVibrationalDunhamEnergy} \cite{Sun2002}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation}
        \resizebox{.9\hsize}{!}{$E_{v,J} = \underbrace{\omega_e \Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}} + B_eJ\Parenthesis{J +
        1}}_{\text{Harmonic}}  - \overbrace{\omega_e x_e\Parenthesis{v +
        \frac{1}{2}}^2 - D_e J^2\Parenthesis{J + 1}^2 }^{\text{Anharmonic}} -
        \underbrace{\alpha_e\Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}} J\Parenthesis{ J +
        1}}_{\text{Vibration-rotation interaction}}$}
    \end{equation}

    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rotVibrationalDunhamEnergy}
        E_{vJ} = \sum_{m,n} Y_{mn}\Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}}^m
        J^n\Parenthesis{J + 1}^n
    \end{equation}

    Some textbooks \cite{Harris2008} use only the
    \textit{Harmonic} terms from \cref{eq:bornOppenheimerApproximation} to
    define the \textit{Hamiltonian}, which
    is a good approximation near the ground state. The vibrational energy for
    the \textit{harmonic} energy equation is given in
    \cref{eq:vibrationalEnergy} for the vibrational term and in
    \cref{eq:rotationalEnergy} for the rotational term. The additional terms come from perturbation theory and are called the \textit{anharmonic}
    terms \cite{Griffiths2018}.
    \begin{subequations}\label{eq:vibrationalRotationalEnergies}
        \begin{align}
            %\varepsilon_{trans} &= \label{eq:tranlationalEnergy} \\
            \varepsilon_{vib} &=
            \omega_e\Parenthesis{v + \frac{1}{2}} \UnitsFrac{1}{cm} \label{eq:vibrationalEnergy} \\
            \varepsilon_{rot} &= B_e J\Parenthesis{J+1} \UnitsFrac{1}{cm}\label{eq:rotationalEnergy}
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    \textit{Kayser} units can be easily converted to energy
    units of \textit{Joules} using \cref{eq:wavenumberToJoules} and to
    \textit{electronvolts} using \cref{eq:wavenumberToElectronvolts}.
    \begin{subequations}\label{eq:wavenumberToEnergy}
        \begin{align}
            \Units{J} &= \UnitsFrac{1}{cm} \times c_0 \UnitsFrac{m}{s} \times
            2\pi\hbar\Units{J\,s} \times 100 \label{eq:wavenumberToJoules} \\
            \Units{eV} &= \UnitsFrac{1}{cm} \times c_0 \UnitsFrac{m}{s} \times
            2\pi\hbar\Units{J\,s} \times 100 \times \frac{1}{q_e} \Units{eV}
            \label{eq:wavenumberToElectronvolts}
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    The \gls{ZPE} was calculated using \cref{eq:zpe} provided in
    \cite{Irikura2007}. The \gls{ZPE} is the lowest possible energy that a
    quantum mechanical system may have. It has to be noted that this is only
    possible to be obtained for diatomic molecules, because monatomic
    molecules only have translational energy modes \cite{Eisberg1985}. 
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:zpe}
        ZPE = \frac{\omega_e}{2} - \frac{\omega_ex_e}{2} + \frac{\omega_ey_e}{8} +
        \frac{B_e}{4} + \frac{\alpha_e\omega_e}{12B_e} +
        \frac{\alpha_e^2\omega_e^2}{144B_e^3}
    \end{equation}

    Some authors refer to the constants in \cref{tab:spectroscopyConstant} 
    as the \textit{Dunham} coefficients \cite{Dunham1932} $\Parenthesis{Y_{ij}
    = Y_{vJ}}$ \cite{Chandra2005}. However, in the literature, these
    coefficients may also be referred to as the \textit{Dunham} potential
    coefficients, which are not the same. Ogilvie \cite{Ogilvie1976} called the
    $Y_{vJ}$ energy-coefficients and $a_m$ the \textit{Dunham} potential-coefficients. These conventions will be used for the remainder of
    this work.\Cref{eq:dunhamEnergyCoefficients} provides a relationship between the
    energy coefficients and the spectroscopy constants found in
    \cref{tab:spectroscopyConstant}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:dunhamEnergyCoefficients}
        \begin{aligned}
            Y_{00} &= ZPE   &  Y_{02} &= D_e        & Y_{11} &= -\alpha_e   &
            Y_{30} &= \omega_e y_e  \\
            Y_{01} &= B_e   &  Y_{10} &= \omega_e   & Y_{20} &= -\omega_ex_e &  \\ 
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}

    The \textit{Dunham} potential coefficients are found using
    \cref{eq:dunhamA0} \cite{Ogilvie1976}, \cref{eq:dunhamA1,eq:dunhamA2}

    \cite{Noorizadeh2004}.
    \begin{subequations}\label{eq:dunhamPotentialCoefficients}
        \begin{align}
            a_0 &= \frac{\omega_e^2}{4B_e} \label{eq:dunhamA0} \\
            a_1 &= -\Parenthesis{1 + \frac{\alpha_e\omega_e}{6B_e^2}} \label{eq:dunhamA1} \\
            a_2 &= \frac{5}{4}a_1^2 -
            \frac{2}{3}\Parenthesis{\frac{\omega_ex_e}{B_e}} \label{eq:dunhamA2} \\

        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    A system is in thermodynamic equilibrium when the entropy of the system
    has reached its maximum possible value. Entropy is a measure of the
    number of possible microscopic configurations that correspond to the
    macroscopic state of the system. At low vibrational and rotational
    quantum numbers, the entropy is lower due to fewer accessible states in
    which energy can be distributed. In contrast, at higher quantum
    numbers, there are more possible "energy buckets" available for energy
    distribution, leading to increased entropy. \\
 
    Temperature, which is defined as the average kinetic energy of the molecules,
    correlates directly with the entropy of a system. At higher temperatures,
    molecules possess more kinetic energy that can be distributed among various
    degrees of freedom, including translational, rotational, and vibrational modes.
    Thus, higher temperatures generally lead to higher entropy as more microscopic
    configurations become accessible. \\

 
    The energy distribution of a system in thermal equilibrium can be
    mathematically described by the Boltzmann distribution. This distribution
    provides the probability of a system occupying a specific energy state,
    showing that states with higher energy are less probable but increasingly
    accessible as temperature rises. Consequently, the Boltzmann distribution
    is foundational in explaining how temperature and entropy are related in a
    system at equilibrium, offering insight into molecular behavior at varying
    energy levels. \\

    The Boltzmann distribution, which describes the particle distribution over
    different energy states at thermal equilibrium, is applied to calculate the
    energy levels as a function of the vibrational quantum number. This
    probability distribution is given in \cref{eq:boltzmannDistribution}.
 
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:boltzmannDistribution}
        p_{i,j} = \frac{g_je^{-\beta E_{v,J}}}{Z} = \frac{\Parenthesis{2J +
        1}e^{-\beta\Parenthesis{\varepsilon_i +
        \varepsilon_j}}}{\sum_{v,J}\Parenthesis{2J +
        1}e^{-\beta\Parenthesis{\varepsilon_v +\varepsilon_J}}}
    \end{equation}
 
    It should be noted that in a system of harmonic oscillators, the
    vibrational energy relaxes through a series of Boltzmann distributions.
    However, this is not the case for anharmonic oscillators
    \cite{Anketell1974}. Therefore, for this approach, it is assumed that the
    system contains only harmonic oscillators and the anharmonic
    $\Parenthesis{\omega_ex_e}$ term in vibrational energy equation REFEQ and
    the anharmonic $\Parenthesis{\alpha_e}$ in the rotational energy equation
    are set to zero.
