\section{Optics Overview \label{ch2:opticsOverview}}
    The origins of optics trace back to the earliest foundations of science, as
    ancient Greek philosophers like Pythagoras, Democritus, Empedocles, Plato,
    Aristotle, and others formulated early theories on the nature of light
    \cite{Hecht2017}. These early thinkers debated whether vision was the
    result of "rays" emitted from the eye or light entering it from external
    sources. In the $17^{th}$ century, Isaac Newton advanced the study of
    optics with his proposal that "rays of light are very small bodies emitted
    from shining substances." Around the same time, Christian Huygens proposed
    an alternative description, suggesting that light is a "wave motion"
    spreading out from its source in all directions \cite{Fowles1989}. Huygens
    also correctly concluded that light slows down upon entering a denser
    medium, a key insight distinguishing his wave theory from Newton's particle
    theory. \\

    The field of optics took a major step forward in the $19^{th}$ century with
James Clerk Maxwell’s development of electromagnetic theory, which provided a
comprehensive description of light as a form of electromagnetic waves. Maxwell’s
equations unified electricity, magnetism, and optics, offering a new
perspective on visible light as part of a broader spectrum of electromagnetic
waves. In the $20^{th}$ century, advancements in \gls{QM} introduced the
concept of \textit{quantized} electromagnetic energy, revealing that electromagnetic
energy can only be exchanged in discrete packets known as \textit{photons}. \\

    Today, the theories of Newton and Huygens are understood to complement one
    another, with Maxwell's theory describing the propagation of light as an
    electromagnetic wave and quantum theory addressing the interaction of light
    and matter, including the processes of absorption and emission
    \cite{Fowles1989}. Together, these theories form the dual wave-particle
    understanding of light, a cornerstone of modern optics.
    \Cref{fig:lightEnergySpectrum} shows the energy spectrum, this work will stay in
    the low visible light, ignoring the electron transitions.
    \begin{figure}[H]
            \makebox[\textwidth][c]{
                \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{chapter2/lightEnergySpectrum}
            }
            \caption{Energy spectrum \cite{energySpectrumImage}}\label{fig:lightEnergySpectrum}
    \end{figure}

    The frequencies of transitions between electronic levels lie in the
    visible and ultraviolet, the vibrational transition in the infrared, and
    the rotation transition in the far infrared \cite{Schwabl2007}. 

    \subsection{Electromagnetism}
    As electromagnetic waves travel through a medium, these waves induce a
    polarization and magnetization on the medium's molecules; leading to the
    formation of dipoles. These dipoles generate their own electric and
    magnetic waves, representing bound charges and bound currents,
    respectively. Consequently, the newly formed wave arising from the combined
    effects of polarization and magnetization, interacts with the incident wave
    of identical frequency but varying propagation velocities
    \cite{Griffiths2017, Jackson1998}. This varying in propagation velocity can be quantify
    by the index of refraction given in \Cref{eq:generalIndexOfRefraction}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:generalIndexOfRefraction}
        n = \frac{c_0}{c_m} \equiv \sqrt{\frac{\mu_m\epsilon_m}{\mu_0\epsilon_0}}
        \approx \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon_m}{\epsilon_0}}
    \end{equation}
    Essentially, the index of refraction is an essential property in optics
    that determines the speed by which light travels through a medium other
    than vacuum. \\

    The electric susceptibility $\chi_e$ quantifies
    the material's ability to become polarized in response to an applied
    electric field. It is a dimensionless parameter that describes how much the
    permittivity of the material $\epsilon_m$ deviates from the permittivity of
    free space $\epsilon_0$. For a linear dielectric, the electric
    susceptibility can be calculated using \Cref{eq:electricSusceptibility}
    \cite{Griffiths2017,Jackson1998}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:electricSusceptibility}
        \chi_e = \frac{\epsilon_m}{\epsilon_0} - 1 \approx n^2 -1  
    \end{equation}
    This approximation holds for non-magnetic materials where $\mu_m \approx
    \mu_0$, and it provides a direct link between the optical and electrical
    properties of a dielectric medium. The electric susceptibility plays a
    crucial role in determining the polarization response of a material and
    influences phenomena such as wave propagation, dispersion, and reflection
    at dielectric interfaces.

    \subsection{Aerodynamic Influence on Optical Fields}
    \Cref{fig:ch2LightRefraction} shows a picture of a material bending a
    beam of light. Inside the material the speed of light is slows down due to
    the interaction of light with atoms and molecules in the material. The same
    effect takes place when a beam of light travels across a denser medium such
    as a boundary layer.
    \pic{0.75}{chapter2/refractionOfLight}{A ray of light refracted in a
        plastic block \cite{refractionPhoto}}{fig:ch2LightRefraction}

    The speed light in a thinner medium is larger than in a denser medium
    \cite{Feynman2011b}. Thus the speed of light in a medium is inversely
    proportional to the medium's density
    \Cref{eq:densitySpeedOfLightProportionaly}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:densitySpeedOfLightProportionaly}
        \rho_{m} \propto \frac{1}{c_m}
    \end{equation}

    As a vehicle moves through a medium, it generates pressure waves. When the
    vehicle travels at speeds faster than the speed of sound in that medium,
    these pressure waves cannot move ahead of the vehicle and start to compress
    and bunch together near the leading edge. This compression creates a sudden
    change in pressure and density known as a shock wave. Shock waves are
    characterized by an abrupt and nearly discontinuous change in the
    properties of the medium, such as pressure, temperature, and density, which
    travel through the medium at supersonic speeds. \Cref{fig:ch2ShockWaves}
    illustrates how the pressure wave expansion changes with varying Mach
    numbers. Shock waves begin to form when the Mach number exceeds unity,
    resulting in different fluid properties, such as density, before and after
    the shock. This density gradient affects the index of refraction.
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \makebox[\textwidth][c]{\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{chapter2/shockWaves}}
        \caption{Shock waves formation}
        \label{fig:ch2ShockWaves}
    \end{figure}

    Optical wave propagation through an inhomogeneous medium where the index of
    refraction varies, results in wavefront deformation, as illustrated in
    \Cref{fig:wavefrontDistortion}. This deformation can significantly degrade
    the performance of optical systems. However, it can also be leveraged as a
    visualization technique, such as the Schlieren method, used by experimental
    fluid researchers. This method exploits variations in the refractive index
    to visualize fluid flow, density gradients, and other phenomena in
    transparent media.\\

    It is important to distinguish between the
    term "near field" as used in this context and its use in physical optics. In
    the current context, "near field" refers to aberrating fluid structures that
    are located very close to the exit aperture of the optical system. In contrast,
    in physical optics, the "near field" refers to a region where the Fresnel
    approximations are applicable, but the Fraunhofer approximations are
    not \cite{Jumper2001}.
    \pic{1.0}{chapter2/wavefrontDistortion}{Planar optical wavefront distorted by a
    variant index of refraction
    flowfield}{fig:wavefrontDistortion}

    In this context, short-path (near-field) aberration is commonly referred to as
    \textit{aero-optics}, which deals with the optical distortions caused by turbulent
    airflows near the optical system, such as those experienced by airborne or
    ground-based sensors. On the other hand, long-path aberrations, which occur
    over extended distances through the atmosphere, are generally referred to as
    "atmospheric propagation" effects. These long-path distortions are typically
    due to atmospheric turbulence, temperature gradients, and other environmental
    factors that cause fluctuations in the index of refraction over the optical
    path \cite{Jumper2001}.\\

    Optical aberrations such as the ones generated by turbulent flows, can be
    measured in terms of the \gls{OPL}, given in
    \Cref{eq:OPL}. 
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:OPL}
        OPL(t,x) = \int_{y_1}^{y_2} n(t,x,y)dy = \sum_{i=0}^N n_i(t,x,y) \cdot \Delta y_i
    \end{equation}
    The \gls{OPL} is the distance that light
    travels in a vacuum in the same time the light travels
    a distance $d$ in a medium. The \gls{OPL} determines the phase change that light
    undergoes as it propagates through a medium \cite{Jenkins1976}. In other
    words, it is the "effective" distance light travels when considering how
    different materials slow down the speed of light relative to its speed in a
    vacuum. The \gls{OPL} is proportional to the time it takes light to
    propagate from point $a$ to point $b$. \\

    Instead of measuring the absolute \gls{OPL}, only the relative difference in
    \gls{OPL} across the aperture is important. The \gls{OPD} is given by
    \Cref{eq:OPD}. The \gls{OPD} directly reflects the variations of the
    wave-front phase errors \cite{Wang2007}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:OPD}
        OPD(t,x) = OPL(t,x) - \overline{OPL}(t)
    \end{equation}
    Note: $\overline{\Parenthesis{\;}}$ denotes the spatial average over the
    aperture.\\ 

    The \gls{WD} can be calculated using \Cref{eq:WD}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:WD}
        WD = OPD + y_{out} 
    \end{equation}
    Where: $y_{out}$ is the location where the undisturbed planar wave is
    located. \\

    The \gls{WD} refers to the deviation of a light wave from its ideal,
    undisturbed wavefront shape as it propagates through a medium. This
    distortion arises due to spatial variations in the refractive index caused
    by factors such as temperature gradients, density fluctuations, turbulence,
    or shock waves in the flow field. Wavefront distortion leads to phase
    errors in the optical wave, resulting in degraded image quality, reduced
    beam focus, and optical aberrations. Quantifying and mitigating wavefront
    distortion is critical in high-speed \gls{AO} and adaptive optics
    applications to maintain system performance.\\

    The time-averaged \textit{Strehl ratio} for a given optical phase variance is given
    by \Cref{eq:strehlRatio}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:strehlRatio}
        \overline{SR}(x) = e^{-\sigma_{\phi}^2}
    \end{equation}
    The \textit{Strehl ratio} is a crucial quantity in optics, as it measures the
degradation of image quality due to wavefront aberrations. It is defined as the
ratio of the peak intensity of the aberrated \gls{PSF} to the
peak intensity of the ideal, diffraction-limited \gls{PSF}. A \textit{Strehl ratio}
of $1$
indicates a perfect optical system with no aberrations, while lower values
indicate increasing levels of distortion. In \gls{AO}, the \textit{Strehl
ratio} is
commonly used to quantify the impact of flow-induced refractive index
fluctuations on optical system performance.\\

    The phase variance is essentially the normalized \gls{OPD} variance and it
    is given by \Cref{eq:phaseVariance}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:phaseVariance}
        \sigma_{\phi}^2 = \Parenthesis{\frac{2\pi OPD_{rms}}{\lambda} }^2
    \end{equation}
The phase variance refers to the variability or fluctuation in the phase of a
light wave. It quantifies how much the phase of the wave changes over time or
across space due to disturbances in the propagation medium. Phase variance is
an important metric in \gls{AO} because it directly impacts the coherence and
quality of the optical signal, leading to image degradation, beam spreading and
loss of focus.\\

    The $OPD_{rms}$ is given by \Cref{eq:opdRMS}
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:opdRMS}
        OPD_{rms} = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N\Parenthesis{OPD_i -
        \overline{OPD} }^2   }
    \end{equation}
    The $OPD_{rms}$ quantifies the quality of an optical system's wavefront by
    measuring the extent to which the wavefront deviates from an ideal
    reference wavefront. Lower values of the $OPD_{rms}$ correspond to
    wavefronts closer to the ideal shape, indicating better optical quality and
    less aberration.\\

    Fluctuation in the fluid fields are not the only cause of distortion in
    \gls{AO}. At high speed flows the nonequilibrium effects play an important
    role. Mackey \cite{Mackey2018, Mackey2019, Mackey2019a} demonstrated that there
    is a discrepancy in the \gls{AO} such as the index of refraction between a
    real and a perfect gas. \\

    \subsection{Polarizability}
    The discrepancy in the \gls{AO} properties between a real and a perfect gas
    in a hypersonic flow is due to polarizability; which refers to a molecule's
    tendency to develop a temporary dipole moment when exposed to an electric
    field. When an electric field is applied, the positively charged nucleus
    and negatively charged electron cloud experience opposing forces, causing a
    slight shift in their positions relative to each other. This shift creates
    an induced dipole moment. \\

    In other words, polarizability measures how easily a molecule's electron cloud
    can be distorted or shifted in response to an electric field \cite{Kittel2004}.
    Larger, more spread-out electron clouds are generally more polarizable because
    they are less tightly bound to the nucleus, making them easier to distort.
    \Cref{fig:ch2Polarizability} illustrates the polarizability of a molecule when
    an electric field is applied to it.
    \pic{1}{chapter2/polarizability}{Polarizability, the tendency of a
    molecule to form a dipole moment when an electric field is
    applied}{fig:ch2Polarizability}

    Polarizability influences the index of refraction by affecting how the
    material interacts with \gls{EM}
    waves (light). When light enters a material, the oscillating electric field
    of the light wave induces a temporary dipoles in the atoms, or molecules in
    the material. This induced dipoles create their own electric field, which
    interfere with the incoming light wave, causing it to slow down as it
    propagates through the material. The degree of slowing down is influenced
    by the material's polarizability.   \\

    The polarizability may be usually separated into three distinct parts:
    electronic, ionic and dipolar as shown in
    \Cref{fig:ch2FrequencyDependencePolarizability}. The electronic
    polarizability arises from the displacement of the electron shell relative
    to the nucleus. The ionic polarizability arises from the displacement of
    charged ions with respect to other ions. The dipolar polarizability arises
    from molecules with a permanent electric dipole moment that can change
    orientation in an applied field \cite{Kittel2004}. 
    \pic{1.5}{chapter2/frequencyDependencePolarizability}{Frequency of the
    several contributions to
    polarizability}{fig:ch2FrequencyDependencePolarizability}

    It has to be noted that polarizability is a tensor but for cases of weak
    electric fields the rotational invariant polarizability
    \Cref{eq:rotationalInvariantPolarizability}, which is one-third of the
    second-rank polarizability tensor is a good approximation. For
    high electric fields (non-linear optics), as well as electric multipole
    moments, the higher-order polarizabilities
    (hyper-polarizabilities), become important \cite{Hohm1986}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:rotationalInvariantPolarizability}
        \alpha = \frac{\alpha_{xx} + \alpha_{yy} + \alpha_{zz}}{3}
    \end{equation}

    Some papers provide the polarizability in \gls{CGS} and call it
    \textit{volumetric} polarizability, some other used \gls{SI} and call it
    \textit{atomic} polarizability. \Cref{eq:cgsToSiPolarizability} can be used
    to convert units between \gls{CGS} and \gls{SI}.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:cgsToSiPolarizability}
        \alpha^{SI}_{[Fm^2]} =
        \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0}{10^6}\alpha^{CGS}_{[cm^3]}
    \end{equation}

    Traditionally polarizability is thought to be a function of
    the frequency of the \gls{EM} wave traveling across the medium. However,
    this is not the full story. Hohm \cite{Hohm1986,Hohm1990} showed a
    temperature dependency on polarizability using interferometric
    measurements.\\

    Rychlewski \cite{Rychlewski1980} showed a dependency in the rotational and
    vibrational quantum numbers in the polarizability constant. Kerl
    \cite{Kerl1992} used these previous results to develop a formulation for a
    temperature dependence polarizability. Kerl developed a curve fitting for
    the polarizability at different frequencies and temperatures
    \Cref{eq:kerlFitting}. \\

    Tropina \cite{Tropina2018, Tropina2020} and Anaya \cite{Anaya2023} have
    demonstrated an influence of vibrational energy modes on polarizability.
    Kerl \cite{Hohm1986} and Buldakov \cite{Buldakov2000,Buldakov2003a,
    Buldakov2003b} have demonstrated a temperature dependency in the
    polarizability. As expected in hypersonic flows, the assumption that
    polarizability is only affected by the frequency of the incoming
    \gls{EM} is not valid anymore.\\

    For a hypersonic flow, the index of refraction will account for the change
    in polarizability by considering the species' number density.
    \Cref{eq:diluteRefractiveIndex} is used to calculated the index of
    refraction in a "dilute medium", and \Cref{eq:denseRefractiveIndex}
    (also known as the \textit{Clausius}-\textit{Mossotti} relation) is used
    to calculate the index of refraction in a "dense medium" \cite{Feynman2011,
    Feynman2011a, Feynman2011b}. 
    \begin{subequations}\label{eq:refractiveIndex}
        \begin{align}
            n - 1 &= \frac{1}{2\epsilon_0}\sum_{i=1}^N\alpha_iN_i
            \label{eq:diluteRefractiveIndex} \\ 
            \frac{n^2 - 1}{n^2 + 2} &=
            \frac{1}{3\epsilon_0}\sum_{i=1}^N\alpha_iN_i
            \label{eq:denseRefractiveIndex}
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}
    Typically, a dilute gas is defined as a gas in which the particles are
    sufficiently far apart that intermolecular interactions can be neglected.
    In this regime, the behavior of the gas can be accurately described by the
    ideal gas law, and molecular collisions are considered infrequent and
    uncorrelated \cite{Daily2018}. \\

    Conversely, a dense gas refers to a gas in which the particle density is
    high enough that intermolecular interactions become significant. These
    interactions—such as \textit{Van der Waals} forces or short-range repulsive
    forces—must be accounted for, as they influence the thermodynamic and
    transport properties of the gas. In dense gases, the ideal gas assumption
    breaks down, and more complex equations of state and models are required to
    capture the effects of particle correlations and collective behavior. \\

    This distinction between dilute and dense gases is important in the
    modeling of optical properties in hypersonic flows, particularly when
    calculating quantities such as the index of refraction or polarizability,
    which may be affected by particle interactions under high-density
    conditions. \\
    
    Some authors \cite{Mandarino1976, Mandarino1981, Wang2023, Miller2022,
    Gupta2023} use the \gls{GD} \Cref{eq:gladstoneDaleConst} constant to
    calculate the index of refraction \Cref{eq:gladstoneDaleRelationship}. This
    constant was first formulated by \textit{Gladstone} \& \textit{Dale} in
    $1864$ and it is heavily used in the field of mineralogist.
    \cite{Mandarino1976}. 
    \begin{subequations}
        \begin{align}
            &K_s =
            \frac{\alpha^{SI}_s}{2\epsilon_0}\Parenthesis{\frac{N_A}{M_s}} \UnitsFrac{m^3}{kg}
            \label{eq:speciesGladstoneDaleConst} \\
            &K_{GD} = \frac{1}{\rho}\sum\limits_{s=1}^N K_s\rho_s \label{eq:gladstoneDaleConst} \\
            &n = \rho K_{GD} + 1 \label{eq:gladstoneDaleRelationship}
        \end{align}
    \end{subequations}

    Some studies on optical propagation with applications to hypersonic
    reentry were performed by Gupta \cite{Gupta2023}. In this work, Gupta
    used the \gls{GD} constant to compare two methods (Snell’s law
    and the optical Lagrange equation) for predicting the propagation of an
    optical signal from the Apollo 11 capsule during reentry. Gupta
    demonstrated that both techniques (Snell’s law and optical Lagrange
    equations) matched well. It was observed in this study that the
    contribution to the refractive index by diatomic nitrogen and oxygen is
    greater upstream than downstream.\\ 

    Some work considering the complex part of the index of refraction
\Cref{eq:totalIndexOfRefraction} by Yang \cite{Yang2023}, using a Photon
Monte Carlo simulation, showed that the complex part of the index of
refraction becomes stronger as the beam propagates over a larger distance.
However, in this study, Yang used an approximation as a function of
wavelength for the \gls{GD} of air. This approximation is
given in \Cref{eq:yangGladstoneDaleApproximation}. In this study, Yang did
not consider nonequilibrium effects but accounted for turbulent effects.
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:yangGladstoneDaleApproximation}
        K_{GD} = 2.2244 \times 10^{-4} \SqBracket{1 + \Parenthesis{\frac{6.7132
        \times 10^{-8}}{\lambda} }^2}
        \UnitsFrac{m^3}{kg}
    \end{equation}
