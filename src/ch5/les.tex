\subsection{Internal Flow}
The results presented below were obtained using the \textit{rhoPimpleFoam}
application from \gls{OpenFOAM}, which is specifically designed for simulating
turbulent flows of compressible fluids. For this study, the one-equation
eddy-viscosity model proposed by Yoshizawa \cite{Yoshizawa1986} was employed to
account for turbulence effects, providing a balance between computational
efficiency and accuracy in capturing turbulent transport phenomena. \\

\Cref{ch5:lesContourVelocityMagnitude} presents the contour plot of velocity
magnitude for the inner flow at a time step of $3.4\Units{s}$. As expected, the
\gls{LES} solution captures more turbulent eddies compared to the \gls{RANS}
solvers, highlighting its ability to resolve finer flow structures.
Additionally, the velocity magnitude is not only a function of spatial
coordinates but also varies with time, reflecting the unsteady nature of the
flow. This temporal dependence is particularly important in accurately
capturing transient flow phenomena, which are often smoothed out in \gls{RANS}
simulations.

\Cref{ch5:lesContourKerlPolarizability} presents the polarizability calculated
using the Kerl approximation \cite{Kerl1992}, as defined in
\cref{eq:kerlFitting}, for a wavelength of $633\Units{nm}$. As expected, the
results indicate that polarizability is dependent not only on the wavelength
but also on temperature. This temperature dependence arises due to variations
in molecular interactions and density fluctuations within the flow, which
influence the medium’s optical properties. Understanding these dependencies is
crucial for accurately modeling aero-optic effects in hypersonic flows.

\Cref{ch5:lesContourIndex} presents the dilute formulation of the index of
refraction, as defined in \cref{eq:diluteRefractiveIndex}, illustrating its
dependence on temperature and laser wavelength. As expected, the index of
refraction varies with temperature due to changes in the local density and
molecular composition of the flow. Additionally, its dependence on wavelength
arises from dispersion effects, where different wavelengths experience varying
degrees of refractive behavior. These variations are critical in aero-optic
applications, as they influence wavefront distortions and signal propagation
through high-speed flow fields.

\Cref{lesContourPermitivity} presents the permittivity contour plot, computed
using \cref{eq:generalIndexOfRefraction}, while
\cref{ch5:lesContourSusceptibility} shows the susceptibility, calculated using
\cref{eq:electricSusceptibility}. As observed, the \gls{EM} properties are
significantly influenced by turbulent fluctuations in the flow, leading to
variations in permittivity and susceptibility that can degrade beam quality.
These effects are particularly relevant in aero-optic applications, where
changes in the optical path due to turbulence can introduce phase distortions
and wavefront aberrations. Schlieren visualization techniques, which leverage
variations in the refractive index to depict density gradients, are well known
for applying this concept to analyze and visualize compressible flows
\cite{Settles2001}.
