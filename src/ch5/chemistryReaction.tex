For this investigation the IRV-2 vehicle, shown in \cref{fig:IRV2} is used.
The IRV-2 vehicle is a sphere-biconic with a nose radius of $1.95\Units{cm}$ and a total
length of $1.386 \Units{m}$. Six drag flaps are located at the aft end of
the vehicle and can be deployed to a maximum angle of $45$ degrees
\cite{Kuntz2001}. For this work, only axisymmetric calculations on the
IRV-2 nosetip are performed. \Cref{fig:IRV2Mesh} shows
the mesh used in this work.

\Cref{fig:contourTranslationTemperatureFrozen} presents contour plots of
translational temperature, while \cref{fig:contourVibrationalTemperatureFrozen}
displays vibrational temperature contours at different Mach numbers for a
frozen flow. As expected, the post-shock temperature increases with Mach number
due to the rapid compression of the flow. In the case of a frozen flow, this
compression occurs as an adiabatic process, where kinetic energy is converted
into thermal energy, leading to a sudden temperature rise. Furthermore, higher
Mach numbers result in proportionally greater increases in both translational
and vibrational temperatures.

\Cref{fig:contourTranslationTemperatureNonequilibrium} presents contour plots
of translational temperature, while
\cref{fig:contourVibrationalTemperatureNonequilibrium} displays vibrational
temperature contours at different Mach numbers for a nonequilibrium flow. As
expected, the translational temperature increases with Mach number, primarily
at the leading edge of the geometry, where most of the kinetic energy of the
flow is converted into internal energy. However, the increase in vibrational
temperature is relatively lower due to nonequilibrium effects, as the process
is no longer adiabatic. Additionally, as expected, the frozen chemistry case
exhibits higher translational and vibrational temperatures since chemical
reactions do not remove energy from the flow.

\Cref{fig:stagnationTemperatures} shows the temperatures along the stagnation
line for different Mach numbers in both frozen and nonequilibrium flow
conditions. As expected, the shock standoff distance in the frozen case is
observed to be larger: approximately $2.3\Units{mm}$ for the slowest and medium
Mach numbers (Cases $1R$ and $2$), and $2\Units{mm}$ for the largest Mach
number (Case $3R$). On the other hand, the nonequilibrium case shows a shock
standoff distance of approximately $2\Units{mm}$ for the slow Mach number (Case
$1R$), $1.7\Units{mm}$ for the medium Mach number (Case $2R$), and
$1.5\Units{mm}$ for the largest Mach number (Case $3R$).\\

This difference arises because when chemistry effects are not considered, the
post-shock density is under-predicted (increasing the density ratio), and the
post-shock temperature is over-predicted (decreasing the temperature ratio).
The shock standoff scales with the density ratio across the shock times the
radius of the vehicle. When chemistry effects are neglected, the density ratio
across the shock is greater, and as a result, the shock standoff distance is
larger compared to when chemistry effects are taken into account.

\Cref{fig:stagnationMassFraction} illustrates the mass fraction of species
along the stagnation line. As expected, in the frozen flow case, there is no
change in mass fraction because there is no energy exchange with the internal
energy modes. However, in the nonequilibrium flow case, energy is exchanged
between internal energy modes, resulting in changes in the mass fractions of
chemical species. \\

For the nonequilibrium case, diatomic nitrogen (\ce{N2}) and diatomic oxygen
(\ce{O2}) dissociate partially, forming nitric oxide (\ce{NO}), atomic oxygen
(\ce{O}), and atomic nitrogen (\ce{N}) after the shock wave. The extent of this
dissociation increases with Mach number due to the higher post-shock
temperatures at greater Mach numbers. Higher temperatures provide the energy
required to overcome the dissociation energy of molecular species, leading to
greater production of \ce{NO}, \ce{O}, and \ce{N}.
